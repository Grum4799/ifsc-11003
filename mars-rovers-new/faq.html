Frequently Asked
Questions

Everything you've wondered about Mars, its rovers, and what comes next.

Category 01

About Mars

How far is Mars from Earth?

Mars' distance from Earth varies significantly due to both planets' orbits around the Sun. At its closest, Mars is about 54.6 million km away; at its farthest, over 400 million km. One-way communication signals take 3 to 22 minutes, and travel time for a spacecraft is roughly 6 to 9 months.

What is the Martian atmosphere like?

Mars has a thin atmosphere made up of about 95% carbon dioxide, with less than 1% of Earth's atmospheric pressure. This makes temperatures extreme — averaging around −60°C — and provides almost no protection from solar radiation or UV rays.

Did Mars ever have water?

Yes. Multiple lines of evidence point to a wetter Mars in the distant past: ancient river valley networks, lake bed sediments, and minerals like sulfates and clays that only form in the presence of liquid water. Rovers have studied these features directly on the surface.

Category 02

The Rovers

How do rovers communicate with Earth?

Rovers communicate using NASA's Deep Space Network — a system of giant radio antennas located in California, Spain, and Australia. They can also relay data through orbiters like Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, which acts as a communication bridge between the rover and Earth. Signal delays range from 3 to 22 minutes each way.

Can rovers be driven in real time?

No. Because signals take several minutes to travel between Earth and Mars, real-time joystick control is impossible. Instead, engineers plan and upload detailed command sequences in advance. Rovers also have onboard hazard-detection software to help avoid obstacles autonomously.

How are rovers powered?

Spirit and Opportunity used solar panels, which limited operations during dust storms and winter. Curiosity and Perseverance are powered by Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generators (MMRTGs), which convert heat from decaying plutonium-238 into electricity — providing steady power regardless of sunlight or dust conditions.

Category 03

Future Exploration

Have we found life on Mars?

Not yet. No mission has confirmed the presence of past or present life. However, Mars shows strong evidence it was once habitable — liquid water, organic molecules, and energy sources are all present in the geological record. The search continues with Perseverance's sample collection, designed to be analyzed in Earth labs with far more powerful instruments.

What happens to Perseverance's rock samples?

Perseverance is caching sealed tubes of Martian rock and regolith at designated depot sites on the surface. A future Mars Sample Return mission — planned as a joint effort between NASA and the European Space Agency — would collect those tubes and bring them back to Earth for detailed laboratory analysis.

When will humans go to Mars?

Various agencies and companies have proposed crewed Mars missions in the 2030s to 2040s. Key challenges include radiation exposure during the 7-month transit, life support, landing heavy payloads, and returning safely. Rover missions like Curiosity and Perseverance are actively gathering the data needed to make those missions safer.
